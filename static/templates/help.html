FAQs

1. What is this website used for?

This website an online demo for distanse-based group contribution (DBGC) method.

2. What is distance-based group contribution (DBGC) method used for?

DBGC method is mainly used for thermodynamic properties prediction. It is a method revised based on group additivity (GA) method [1].

This website is just an illustration for DBGC method. Only prediction of standard enthalpy of formation for alkane and alkene, and their radical are supported currently. In the future, more research will be conducted on the prediction of entropy and heat capacity, as well as the enthanlpy of other classes of species.

3. What is group additivity (GA) method?

Originally proposed by Benson and Buss [2] in 1958, the group additivity (GA) method treats a molecular property as being composed of contributions based on groups. The main idea is that even if there are millions of chemical species of interest, the number of functional groups constituting these compounds is much smaller.

Due to the large amount of chemical species in detailed reaction mechanisms, experimental measurements or high-level quantum calculations of the thermodynamic properties for each species can be a daunting task. Typically, in existing large combustion mechanisms containing thousands of species, a considerable amount of thermodynamic data is derived from the Benson-type [1,3] GA values.

4. What is the advantages of DBGC method?

The DBGC method is more systematic, automatic, and adaptive then the conventional GA method. In the newly developed DBGC method, the interaction between two groups is characterized with an exponential decay function of their distance, i.e., the number of bonds in-between in the molecular structure. In this way, all intramolecular interactions are described in an uniform rule, avoiding manually selecting some sub-structures to represent the local complexity around a group, which is the approach used in conventioanl GA method. Because human effort is not necessary to construct the group library, species with new groups or new sub-structures can be added into the database easily, and the parameters in the algorithm can be retrained in a short time frame without changing the code. The DBGC is also potential to produce higher accuracy over the conventional GA method, especially for highly branched hydrocarbons. A comparison of accuracy between the DBGC method and the conventional GA method has been conducted on alkanes. More details can be found in the paper [4].

5. What's the limitation of DBGC method?

The DBGC method relies on large amount of data for learning from various structures. Generally, thermodynamic data can be obtained from experiments or high-level theoretical computations. However, experimental measurements usually demand too much manpower, time, and cost. The existing experimental standard enthalpy of formation for large molecules, e.g. alkanes with more than 10 carbon atoms, and radicals are still rare. Therefore, quantum chemistry computation methods were used to construct the database. Our vision is that in the near future it will be much easier to obtain relatively accurate data, because of the rapid development of computer technology and the quick reduction of cost.

7. How does DBGC work?

Firstly, the input molecular scratch will be translated into the connectivity information. Then the connectivity infomation will be translated into the amount of each type of groups and some exponential decay functions of group distances. The contribution of groups in divided into two parts in the DBGC method. The amount of each type of groups is mapped to the contribution from individual groups, and the exponential decay functions is mapped to the contribution from interactions between groups. In the mapping process, sources and targets are related by parameters obtained from multiple linear regression (MLR) and artificial neural network (ANN) fitting with a molecule set, the themodynamic property of which is known. And the predicted thermodynamic property is the summation of these two parts of contribution. More details can be found in the paper [4].

8. What is used as the database currently?

A database with information of about 925 alkanes (C3-C13), 634 alkenes (C3-C10), 871 alkyl radicals (C3-C10), and 1458 alkenyl radicals (C3-C9) was constructed at the M06-2X/def2-TZVP//B3LYP/6-31G(d) level of theory. The default training set consists of 305 alkanes (C3-C11), 113 alkenes (C3-C8), 364 alkyl radicals (C3-C9), and 546 alkenyl radicals (C3-C8) to parameter the algorithm. The parameterized algorithm was valid with a test set consisting of 620 alkanes (C12-C13), 521 alkenes (C9-C10), 507 alkyl radicals (C10) and 912 alkenyl radicals (C9). The unsaturate species in the database have only one double bond each. The database can be downloaded at .github. (link)

9. How to construct my own database?

The key data needed is the thermodynamic data and connecitivy infomation. If using computational method to obtain the thermodynamic data, some code to assit the process of conformational analysis and quantum chemistry computation result aftertreatment can be downloaded at .github (link).
